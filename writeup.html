Mapping the news: unsupervised topic discovery across 7,000 articles

This project builds an interactive 3D semantic map of 7,000 news articles from 2018, using sentence embeddings, UMAP dimensionality reduction, and HDBSCAN clustering to surface latent topic structure without any labelled data. The result is a fully static visualization deployed on GitHub Pages where users can explore clusters, filter by date, and read source articles.

1. introduction

Keyword search treats words as discrete symbols, which means that two articles about the same event can fail to match if they use different vocabulary. Semantic search addresses this by operating in a learned embedding space where proximity reflects meaning rather than surface form. This project asks whether that same embedding space, when applied to a large news corpus, naturally organises itself into interpretable topics without any supervision.

The short answer is yes. Without providing a single label, the pipeline recovers coherent clusters corresponding to events like the Cambridge Analytica scandal, the US-China trade war, the Stanley Cup playoffs, and 24 other distinct topics, all from the geometric structure of the embeddings alone.

2. data

Articles were drawn from the CC-News dataset via HuggingFace, filtered to the first half of 2018, and cleaned to remove duplicates and near-empty titles. The final corpus contains 7,000 articles spanning January to July 2018, sourced from a wide range of English-language publishers. Only article titles were used for embedding rather than full text. This was a deliberate choice: titles are more consistent in length and register across sources, and in practice they carry enough semantic signal to separate topics cleanly.

3. method

Each title was embedded using all-MiniLM-L6-v2 from the sentence-transformers library, producing a 384-dimensional vector per article. These embeddings were then reduced to three dimensions using UMAP with cosine distance, n_neighbors=20, and min_dist=0.1. Three dimensions were chosen over the more common two because the additional axis preserves more of the original embedding structure, as confirmed by the trustworthiness scores in Section 4.

Clustering was performed using HDBSCAN with min_cluster_size=60 and min_samples=10. HDBSCAN was preferred over k-means for two reasons: it does not require specifying the number of clusters in advance, and it explicitly models noise points rather than forcing every article into a cluster. Points assigned label -1 are treated as noise and rendered as grey dots in the visualization.

Cluster labels were generated automatically using TF-IDF over the concatenated titles within each cluster, extracting the top three most distinctive unigrams and bigrams relative to the rest of the corpus.

4. results

The pipeline produced 27 clusters from 7,000 articles, with 3,525 points (50.4%) assigned as noise. Selected clusters are shown below.

Evaluation used three metrics. DBCV measures cluster cohesion and is designed specifically for density-based methods. Trustworthiness measures how well the low-dimensional projection preserves local neighbourhood structure from the original 384-dimensional space.

method trustworthiness DBCV

UMAP 3D achieves the highest trustworthiness score of the three methods tested, suggesting that the extra dimension meaningfully captures neighbourhood structure that is lost in 2D projections. The t-SNE and UMAP 2D scores were computed on a 2,000-point subsample for computational reasons, so the comparison is indicative rather than exact.

5. discussion

The most coherent clusters correspond to events with a distinctive and consistent vocabulary, such as the Cambridge Analytica scandal (cluster 01) and the North Korea nuclear negotiations (cluster 02). Clusters around broader or more diffuse topics, such as general crime reporting (cluster 16), tend to be larger and produce less informative TF-IDF labels, which reflects a genuine limitation of title-only embedding rather than a failure of the clustering method.

The noise rate of 50.4% is higher than ideal but not surprising for a general news corpus. Many articles cover one-off events that do not belong to any recurring topic, and HDBSCAN correctly identifies these as noise rather than forcing them into a cluster. This is one of the key advantages of the method over k-means.

One limitation worth noting is that the TF-IDF labels are sometimes dominated by source-specific terms rather than topic terms. Cluster 21, labelled "di | dan | yang", appears to consist largely of articles from Chinese-language sources translated into English, which suggests that source domain can be a stronger signal than topic in some cases.

Future work could extend this to a larger corpus spanning multiple years, which would allow tracking of how topics evolve over time. Replacing title-only embedding with full article embedding using a larger model such as all-mpnet-base-v2 would likely improve cluster quality, at the cost of higher compute.
